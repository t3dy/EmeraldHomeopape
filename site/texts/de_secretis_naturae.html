LATIN · TRANSLATION · 1145–1151 CE

De secretis naturae

De secretis naturae (On the Secrets of Nature), also known as the Liber de secretis naturae, is the first known Latin translation of the complete Arabic Kitab Sirr al-Khaliqa (Book of the Secret of Creation). It was produced by Hugo of Santalla, a twelfth-century translator active in the Iberian Peninsula, as part of the broader movement of Arabic-to-Latin translation that transformed European intellectual culture.

Hugo's translation preserves the cosmological context and frame narrative of the Arabic original, including the account of Balinas's discovery of the Emerald Tablet in a subterranean vault. Notably, Hugo's Latin rendering of the Tablet's most famous axiom differs from the later vulgate version: he translates the principle of correspondence as 'Superiora de inferioribus, inferiora de superioribus' (the higher things from the lower, the lower from the higher), rather than the vulgate's 'Quod est inferius est sicut quod est superius' (what is below is like what is above).

Despite being the earliest complete Latin witness to the Sirr al-Khaliqa tradition, Hugo's translation did not achieve the widespread circulation of either the vulgate Emerald Tablet or the Secretum Secretorum. Its relative obscurity means that most medieval European engagement with the Tablet was mediated through later, more streamlined versions.

De secretis naturae is nonetheless indispensable for textual scholars and historians of Hermeticism because it preserves the full cosmological framework within which the Emerald Tablet was originally embedded, allowing comparison with the standalone vulgate text and illuminating how the Tablet was progressively decontextualized as it moved through European intellectual culture.
