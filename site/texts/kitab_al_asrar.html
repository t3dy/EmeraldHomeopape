Book of Secrets (Kitab al-Asrar) TREATISE

ARABIC · TREATISE · 850–925 CE

كتاب الأسرار

The Kitab al-Asrar (Book of Secrets), also known as the Kitab Sirr al-Asrar in some manuscript traditions, is an Arabic alchemical treatise attributed to Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn Zakariyya al-Razi (ca. 854-925 CE), known in the Latin West as Rhazes. Al-Razi was one of the most important physicians and alchemists of the Islamic Golden Age, and this work represents a major contribution to practical alchemical literature.

The Book of Secrets describes chemical operations, laboratory equipment, and procedures for the classification and transmutation of substances. It synthesizes Greek chemical philosophy, particularly the theory of matter inherited from Aristotelian and Hellenistic sources, with the empirical experimental methods that characterized the Arabic alchemical tradition. The work is notable for its systematic and relatively pragmatic approach to alchemical practice compared to the more allegorical treatments found in many contemporary texts.

The treatise occupies an important position in the history of alchemy as a bridge between the theoretical Hermetic tradition and the development of experimental chemistry. Al-Razi's work was studied by Julius Ruska in the early twentieth century, whose critical editions and analyses helped establish the text's significance for the history of science. The Book of Secrets contributed to the broader transmission of alchemical knowledge from the Islamic world to medieval Europe through Latin translations, helping to shape the practical dimension of Western alchemical practice.
