LATIN · PSEUDO_EPIGRAPHA · 1100–1200 CE

De sex rerum principiis

De sex rerum principiis (On the Six Principles of Things) is a medieval Latin treatise attributed to Hermes Trismegistus, dealing with six fundamental cosmological principles. The text adapts Neoplatonic metaphysics for a medieval Latin readership, presenting a hierarchical account of the structure of reality. It has been critically edited by Paolo Lucentini and Mark Damien Delp as part of the Hermes Latinus research project.

The treatise belongs to the extensive body of pseudepigraphical Hermetic literature that circulated in medieval Europe under the name of Hermes. Unlike the philosophical Hermetica of the Corpus Hermeticum, which were unknown in the Latin West until Ficino's translation, texts like De sex rerum principiis formed part of a distinctly medieval Hermetic tradition that drew on Arabic intermediaries and adapted late antique cosmological ideas to Scholastic intellectual frameworks.

The work is significant for understanding the medieval Latin Hermetic tradition as a phenomenon independent of the Renaissance rediscovery of the Greek Corpus Hermeticum. It demonstrates that Hermeticism maintained a continuous, if fragmented, presence in Western intellectual culture throughout the medieval period, transmitted through a network of pseudepigraphical texts that circulated alongside works of natural philosophy, alchemy, and astrology.
