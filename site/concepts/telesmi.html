Telesmi / Telesmum LINGUISTIC

telesmus, ṭilasm, talisman, perfection (Newton)

Transliterated Arabic term (abu al-tilasmat, 'father of talismans') that entered Latin untranslated and became one of the most disputed words in alchemical exegesis.

Telesmi (telesmum) is a Latin transliteration of Arabic abu al-tilasmat ('father of talismans'), appearing in the earliest Latin Emerald Tablet versions. Early translators like Plato of Tivoli transliterated rather than translated, preserving an opaque loanword retaining its link to the talismanic magical tradition.

The word's untranslated state generated centuries of controversy. Latin readers could not determine whether telesmi meant talismans, perfections, or initiations. Newton replaced it with 'perfection,' severing the talismanic connection. Other translators offered different solutions, each encoding different understandings of the Tablet's purpose.

Telesmi represents an extreme case of the Decknamen phenomenon: a cover name whose referent was lost not through deliberate concealment but through cross-linguistic transmission. It demonstrates how the Arabic-to-Latin translation process actively created new layers of interpretive difficulty, producing a text whose opacity was partly an artifact of its own transmission history.

Significance

Telesmi demonstrates how the Latin tradition created interpretive difficulty through transliteration rather than translation. The word's opacity generated competing readings that reveal the fundamental instability of the Emerald Tablet's meaning across its transmission history.
