Sulfur-Mercury Theory ALCHEMICAL

chemical wedding, Sol and Luna

The doctrine that all metals are composed of philosophical sulfur (active masculine principle) and philosophical mercury (passive feminine principle).

The sulfur-mercury theory holds that all metals are composed of two philosophical principles: sulfur (active, hot, masculine, solar) and mercury (passive, cold, feminine, lunar). Metals differ only in proportion and purity of these constituents; transmutation requires purifying and recombining them in ideal balance to produce gold.

Hortulanus's fourteenth-century commentary decoded the Emerald Tablet through this lens: the Sun as philosophical sulfur, the Moon as mercury, wind as vaporous circulation within the sealed vessel, earth as fixation through solid ferment. This reading transformed the Tablet's cosmological pronouncements into a precise laboratory recipe.

The theory bridges Hermetic cosmology and practice by mapping macrocosm-microcosm correspondence onto specific operations: just as the cosmos was generated from active and passive cosmic principles, so the Philosopher's Stone is generated from purified sulfur and mercury. Newton continued this interpretive tradition in his own commentary.

Significance

The sulfur-mercury theory is the dominant interpretive framework through which the Emerald Tablet was read as practical instruction from Hortulanus through Newton, translating Hermetic cosmological symbolism into operative alchemical procedure.
