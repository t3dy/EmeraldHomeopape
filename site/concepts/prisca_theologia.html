Prisca Theologia (Ancient Theology) PHILOSOPHICAL

perennial philosophy, philosophia perennis

The Renaissance doctrine that a single divine truth was given to humanity in antiquity and passed through a chain of sages including Hermes, Moses, Orpheus, Pythagoras, and Plato.

Prisca theologia (ancient theology) is the Renaissance doctrine that a single divine truth was revealed at the dawn of history and transmitted through a chain of sages: Hermes Trismegistus, Moses, Orpheus, Pythagoras, and Plato. Ficino, translating the Corpus Hermeticum in 1463, positioned Hermes as the first link in this golden chain of wisdom.

The doctrine served a crucial strategic function: by framing Hermetic texts as anticipations of Christian revelation, Ficino and Pico created space for studying Egyptian and Greek esoteric traditions without charges of heresy. Prisca theologia made Hermeticism pious supplementation rather than pagan competition.

Casaubon's 1614 redating of the Hermetica, showing them to be early CE compositions rather than Mosaic-era texts, undermined the chronological foundation. Yet the idea proved resilient: the conviction that ancient traditions preserve genuine spiritual truths continued to animate Hermetic, Rosicrucian, and Masonic movements into modernity.

Significance

Prisca theologia was the intellectual framework making Renaissance Hermeticism culturally possible. Without it, studying pagan Egyptian texts attributed to Hermes would have been condemned as heresy rather than celebrated as confirming Christianity's ancient pedigree.
