Theurgy (Divine Work) THEOLOGICAL

θεουργία, sacred ritual

Sacred ritual designed to enact union with the divine through participatory action rather than coercive manipulation.

Theurgy ('divine work') designates sacred ritual aimed at divine union through participatory action rather than coercive manipulation. Distinguished from goetia (sorcery compelling spirits), theurgy requires the practitioner's moral preparation and operates through voluntary descent of divine presence into consecrated materials, names, and symbols.

Iamblichus's De mysteriis provides the foundational defense. Against Porphyry's objection that contemplation alone should suffice, Iamblichus argues the soul's fallen state makes purely intellectual ascent impossible; gods must descend through ritual means including ineffable names, synthemata, and timed ceremonies. As Fraser shows, Iamblichus grounds this explicitly in Hermetic priestly traditions.

Theurgy bridges philosophical and operative Hermeticism, connecting nous, logos, and emanation to concrete ritual practice. The Emerald Tablet's instructions for separation, purification, and recombination can themselves be read as theurgic operations transforming both substance and practitioner simultaneously.

Significance

Theurgy bridges Hermetic philosophical theology and operative practice. Iamblichus's defense of theurgic ritual in De mysteriis, grounded explicitly in Hermetic priestly tradition, is the key text linking Hermetic thought to Neoplatonic metaphysics and ritual action.
