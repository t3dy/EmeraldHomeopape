Barbarous Names (Onomata Barbara) LINGUISTIC

sacred language, ὀνόματα βαρβαρικά

Foreign, non-Greek sacred names carrying inherent divine energy that cannot survive translation.

Barbarous names (onomata barbara) are foreign, non-Greek sacred utterances held to carry inherent divine power that cannot survive translation. The concept includes unintelligible ritual formulas like ABLANATHANALBA from the Greek magical papyri and meaningful epithets of gods preserved in Egyptian or Assyrian rather than rendered into Greek.

Iamblichus defends barbarous names in De mysteriis (7.4-5), arguing they are ontologically joined to divine beings and possess elevating power that bypasses human conceptualization. Fraser demonstrates this defense draws on Hermetic priestly traditions, with shared vocabulary between CH XVI and De mysteriis revealing a common Egyptian hieratic source.

The doctrine connects to the broader Hermetic understanding of language as energetic rather than conventional. It undergirds theurgic ritual and illuminates the Emerald Tablet's transmission, where terms like telesmi survived untranslated because translators intuited that rendering them into Latin would diminish their potency.

Significance

Barbarous names embody a theory of ritual language as ontologically potent rather than merely referential. This doctrine bridges Hermetic theology and Neoplatonic theurgy, providing the basis for why sacred utterances and material symbols can effect genuine transformation.
