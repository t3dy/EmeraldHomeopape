Jabir ibn Hayyan AUTHOR

8th-9th c. CE · 721–815 · AUTHOR

Also known as: Geber, جابر بن حيان

Jabir ibn Hayyan (fl. eighth–ninth century CE) is the foundational figure of Islamic alchemy, credited with a massive corpus of hundreds of treatises covering chemistry, medicine, cosmology, and philosophy. Modern scholarship, particularly the work of Paul Kraus, has demonstrated that the Jabirian corpus was likely a collective pseudepigraphical effort, possibly produced by writers of Ismaili persuasion over an extended period.

Jabir's significance for the Hermetic tradition is substantial. The Emerald Tablet appears in the Kitab Ustuqus al-Uss al-Thani (Second Book of the Elements of Foundation), one of the texts attributed to him. This constitutes one of the earliest known Arabic witnesses to the Tablet, identified by E.J. Holmyard in the early twentieth century. The Jabirian corpus also engages extensively with the figure of Balinas (Apollonius of Tyana), connecting the Tablet's teachings to a broader framework of alchemical theory.

The Jabirian writings synthesized Greek natural philosophy, Hermetic cosmology, and practical chemical experimentation into a comprehensive alchemical system. The corpus introduced the influential theory of the balance (mizan), which sought to quantify the properties of substances. Early authorities including al-Razi and Ibn Umayl acknowledged Jabir's importance, and his works were widely cited throughout the medieval Islamic world and, in Latin translation, in the medieval West.
