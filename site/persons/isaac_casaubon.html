Isaac Casaubon SCHOLAR

late 16th-early 17th c. CE · 1559–1614 · SCHOLAR

Isaac Casaubon (1559–1614) was a Franco-Swiss classical scholar and one of the greatest philologists of the early modern period. His 1614 analysis of the Corpus Hermeticum in De rebus sacris et ecclesiasticis exercitationes fundamentally altered the intellectual history of Hermeticism.

Through linguistic and stylistic analysis, Casaubon demonstrated that the Corpus Hermeticum could not be the work of an ancient Egyptian sage contemporaneous with Moses. He identified post-Christian vocabulary, Platonic concepts, and stylistic features placing the texts in the early centuries CE. He noted the mention of the sculptor Phidias as evidence against an archaic date and pointed to parallels with New Testament language.

Casaubon's dating dismantled the Renaissance doctrine of prisca theologia—the belief that Hermes Trismegistus was a historical prophet who anticipated Christian revelation. Ficino and Pico della Mirandola had built philosophical systems on this assumption. While Casaubon did not deny that a sage named Hermes might have existed, he regarded the extant texts as later compositions.

Remarkably, Casaubon's critique did not end Hermetic influence. The alchemical Hermetica, including the Emerald Tablet, were largely unaffected, and Hermetic ideas continued to shape esoteric and scientific thought well into the seventeenth century.
