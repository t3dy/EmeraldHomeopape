Balinas (Pseudo-Apollonius of Tyana) MYTHICAL_FIGURE

1st c. CE (historical) / 8th-9th c. (textual persona) · MYTHICAL_FIGURE

Also known as: Apollonius of Tyana, بلينس, Apollonius Tyanaeus

Balinas is the Arabic rendering of Apollonius of Tyana, a historical neo-Pythagorean philosopher of the first century CE who was transformed in Arabic literary culture into a legendary sage and master of talismanic arts. Known in Arabic sources as sahib al-tilasmat (Lord of the Talismans), Balinas became the pseudepigraphic author of the Kitab Sirr al-Khaliqa wa San'at al-Tabi'a (Book of the Secret of Creation and the Art of Nature), one of the most important texts in the Arabic Hermetic tradition.

Balinas's principal significance for the Hermetic tradition lies in his role as the attributed discoverer and transmitter of the Emerald Tablet (Tabula Smaragdina). In the frame narrative of the Sirr al-Khaliqa, Balinas recounts entering a subterranean vault where he discovers the tomb of Hermes Trismegistus, seated on a golden throne and holding an emerald tablet inscribed with the secrets of nature. This discovery narrative, which Ursula Weisser's critical edition (1979) established as deriving from a late antique Greek original possibly mediated through Syriac, provided the foundational context for the Tablet's transmission into Arabic and subsequently Latin intellectual culture.

The figure of Balinas thus serves as a critical hinge between the Hellenistic Hermetic tradition and its Arabic reception. The Sirr al-Khaliqa, with its cosmological encyclopedia structured around the six days of creation and its appended Emerald Tablet, shaped the understanding of Hermetic wisdom throughout the medieval Islamic world and, through Latin translations by Hugo of Santalla and others, in the Latin West as well.
