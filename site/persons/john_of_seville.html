John of Seville TRANSLATOR

12th c. CE · TRANSLATOR

Also known as: Johannes Hispalensis

John of Seville (Johannes Hispalensis, fl. c. 1120–1150) was a twelfth-century translator active in the Iberian Peninsula who played an important role in the transmission of Arabic learning to the Latin West. He was part of the broader translation movement centered on Toledo and other Iberian cities.

His principal contribution to the Hermetic tradition was his translation of an abridged version of the pseudo-Aristotelian Secretum Secretorum (Sirr al-Asrar) from Arabic into Latin around 1120–1140. His translation, titled Epistula ad Alexandrum de dieta servanda or De regimine sanitatis, focused primarily on medical and astrological content from the shorter Arabic recension of the work.

Notably, John of Seville's short version did not contain the section on alchemy and therefore did not include the Emerald Tablet. The full Latin translation that incorporated the Tablet was later completed by Philip of Tripoli around 1220–1243. Nevertheless, John's translation helped establish the Secretum Secretorum as a major text in medieval Latin intellectual culture and prepared the ground for the wider dissemination of its Hermetic and alchemical content.
