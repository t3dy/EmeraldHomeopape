Marsilio Ficino TRANSLATOR

15th c. CE · 1433–1499 · TRANSLATOR

Also known as: Ficino

Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499) was the Florentine Neoplatonist philosopher, priest, and translator who stands as the central figure in the Renaissance revival of Hermeticism. The founder of the Platonic Academy in Florence, he worked under the patronage of Cosimo de' Medici and shaped the intellectual landscape of the Italian Renaissance.

Ficino's most consequential act for the Hermetic tradition was his 1463 Latin translation of the Corpus Hermeticum, completed at Cosimo's request before Ficino turned to his translation of Plato—a priority that reflects the extraordinary esteem in which Hermetic wisdom was held. Ficino believed Hermes Trismegistus was a historical Egyptian sage, a prisca theologia figure who had preceded and anticipated both Plato and Moses.

His major original work, the Platonic Theology (Theologia Platonica), integrated Hermetic themes—divine nous, the cosmic sympathy between above and below, the soul's ascent through the planetary spheres—into a comprehensive philosophical system that harmonized Neoplatonism with Christianity. He also retitled Iamblichus's reply to Porphyry as De mysteriis Aegyptiorum, shaping all subsequent reception of that text.

Ficino's translations and philosophical synthesis made the Hermetica available to the entire educated world of Renaissance Europe. His vision of an ancient theological tradition linking Egypt, Greece, and Christianity dominated European intellectual life until Casaubon's philological critique of 1614.
