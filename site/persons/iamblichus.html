Iamblichus PHILOSOPHER

late 3rd-early 4th c. CE · 245–325 · PHILOSOPHER

Also known as: Iamblichus of Chalcis, Abamon

Iamblichus (c. 245–325 CE) was a Syrian Neoplatonic philosopher and theurgist. A student of Porphyry, he broke with his teacher's rationalist approach to religion, becoming the foremost defender of traditional cultic practices in the late antique Mediterranean.

His principal contribution to the Hermetic tradition lies in the De mysteriis, written under the pseudonym Abamon, an Egyptian prophetic persona. Iamblichus defended theurgy—invoking divine presence through ritual, sacred names, and material offerings—against Porphyry's critique in the Letter to Anebo. He argued that contemplation alone was insufficient for salvation and that the gods must descend through hieratic traditions embodying divine presence.

Iamblichus treated the Hermetic writings as authentic translations of the Egyptian Books of Thoth, asserting that their Greek philosophical vocabulary did not diminish their Egyptian authority. His defense of barbarous names in ritual invocation parallels arguments in Corpus Hermeticum XVI. Kyle Fraser has identified shared vocabulary between the De mysteriis and the Hermetica, including the rare term exitalos.

Iamblichus remains pivotal for understanding the intersection of Neoplatonism, Hermeticism, and Egyptian priestly religion. His justification of ritual practice influenced both Byzantine and Renaissance esoteric traditions.
