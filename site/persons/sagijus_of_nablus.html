Sagijus of Nablus AUTHOR

uncertain · AUTHOR

Also known as: Sergius of Nablus, Sajiyus

Sagijus of Nablus (also rendered as Sergius; dates uncertain) is a figure named in an introductory preface to one of the Arabic recensions of the Emerald Tablet, discovered by Julius Ruska in the early twentieth century. According to this twelfth-century recension, the Tablet was dictated by a Christian priest named Sagijus.

Scholars have suggested that Sagijus may preserve a historical memory of Sergius of Reshaina (d. 536 CE), a sixth-century Syriac physician and translator who rendered Greek philosophical and medical works into Syriac. If this identification is correct, it would provide further evidence for a Syriac intermediary stage in the transmission of Hermetic and alchemical knowledge from late antique Greek sources into Arabic.

The figure of Sagijus remains enigmatic, and the historical connection to Sergius of Reshaina is conjectural. Nevertheless, the attribution is significant for the textual history of the Emerald Tablet, as it points to the complex chain of translation and transmission through which this seminal Hermetic text reached the Arabic-speaking world.
