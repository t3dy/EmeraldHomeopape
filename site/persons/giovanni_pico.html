Giovanni Pico della Mirandola PHILOSOPHER

late 15th c. CE · 1463–1494 · PHILOSOPHER

Also known as: Pico

Giovanni Pico della Mirandola (1463-1494) was a Florentine humanist philosopher whose ambitious program of intellectual synthesis radicalized the Hermetic revival initiated by Marsilio Ficino by adding Christian Kabbalah to the already potent mixture of Hermetic, Neoplatonic, and Christian thought. Born into the Italian nobility and prodigiously talented, Pico studied at Bologna, Ferrara, and Padua before arriving in Florence, where he joined Ficino's Platonic circle.

Pico's 900 Theses (Conclusiones nongentae, 1486), intended for public disputation in Rome, represented an audacious attempt to demonstrate the fundamental concordance of all philosophical and theological traditions -- Hermetic, Platonic, Aristotelian, Kabbalistic, Chaldean, and Christian. His celebrated Oration on the Dignity of Man, composed as the intended preface to the disputation, draws directly on Hermetic anthropology, presenting the human being as a protean creature placed at the center of the cosmic hierarchy with the capacity to ascend to the divine or descend to the bestial.

Pico's legacy for the Hermetic tradition is twofold. First, his synthesis of Hermeticism with Kabbalah created a new and immensely influential compound -- Christian Cabala -- that would shape European esotericism for centuries through figures such as Reuchlin, Agrippa, and Dee. Second, his bold claims for the antiquity and authority of Hermetic wisdom, later undermined by Casaubon's philological critique, established the high-water mark of Renaissance confidence in the prisca theologia tradition.
