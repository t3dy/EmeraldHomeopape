Hugo of Santalla TRANSLATOR

12th c. CE · TRANSLATOR

Also known as: Hugo Sanctallensis

Hugo of Santalla (fl. c. 1145-1151) was a twelfth-century Spanish translator working in the Ebro valley who produced the first known complete Latin translation of the Kitab Sirr al-Khaliqa (Book of the Secret of Creation), rendered as De secretis naturae. He belongs to the remarkable generation of translators who, working in the multicultural environment of the Iberian Peninsula, mediated the transfer of Arabic scientific and philosophical learning into the Latin intellectual world.

Hugo's translation of the Sirr al-Khaliqa is significant because it preserved the Arabic frame narrative -- including the discovery account in which Balinas enters the tomb of Hermes and finds the Emerald Tablet -- in Latin for the first time. This distinguished his version from the competing transmission of the Tablet through the Secretum Secretorum tradition, which embedded the Tablet in a pseudo-Aristotelian context and ultimately achieved wider circulation through Philip of Tripoli's translation.

Hugo's legacy in the history of the Emerald Tablet's transmission is that of an important but ultimately less influential channel. While his De secretis naturae did not achieve the wide manuscript dissemination of the Secretum Secretorum version, it preserved crucial contextual material about the Tablet's Arabic literary setting that would otherwise have been lost to Latin readers. Modern scholars of the Tablet's textual history, including Julius Ruska, have relied on Hugo's translation as an essential witness to the fuller Arabic tradition.
