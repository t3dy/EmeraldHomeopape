Albertus Magnus COMMENTATOR

13th c. CE · 1200–1280 · COMMENTATOR

Also known as: Albert the Great

Albertus Magnus (c. 1200-1280), the Dominican friar, bishop of Regensburg, and one of the foremost scholastic philosophers of the thirteenth century, played a crucial role in integrating Hermetic material into the framework of Latin Christian natural philosophy. Known as Doctor Universalis for the extraordinary breadth of his learning, Albert engaged with the full range of Aristotelian and pseudo-Aristotelian texts then available in Latin translation, including works of Hermetic provenance.

Albert's most significant engagement with the Hermetic tradition appears in his Book of Minerals (De mineralibus), where he cited the Emerald Tablet and discussed alchemical doctrines within the context of Aristotelian natural philosophy. His treatment helped legitimize Hermetic ideas within the intellectual culture of the medieval universities, presenting them not as illicit magic but as part of the study of natural causes. Fragments and testimonies regarding Hermes Trismegistus attributed to Albert are preserved in the Hermetica II collection.

Albert's legacy for the Hermetic tradition lies in his role as a bridge figure. By citing Hermetic texts alongside Aristotle and the Church Fathers, he helped ensure that the Tabula Smaragdina and related alchemical doctrines circulated within the mainstream of scholastic thought, preparing the ground for the more extensive alchemical commentaries of his student Thomas Aquinas's circle and the later medieval tradition of Emerald Tablet commentary exemplified by Hortulanus.
