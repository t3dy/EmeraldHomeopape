Al-Razi (Rhazes) AUTHOR

9th-10th c. CE · 854–925 · AUTHOR

Also known as: Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn Zakariyya al-Razi, Rhazes

Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn Zakariyya al-Razi (c. 854-925 CE), known in the Latin West as Rhazes, was one of the most significant figures of the Islamic Golden Age, active as both a physician and alchemist in Baghdad and Rayy. He stands as a pivotal figure in the transition from Hellenistic alchemical theory to systematic Arabic experimentation, synthesizing Greek chemical philosophy with empirical laboratory practice at a time when the Arabic-Islamic world was the primary custodian of ancient scientific knowledge.

Al-Razi's principal contribution to the Hermetic and alchemical tradition is his Book of Secrets (Kitab al-Asrar), a practical manual of alchemical operations that organized substances into a systematic classification of minerals, vitriols, boraxes, salts, and metals. This work, studied and translated by Julius Ruska in the early twentieth century, represents one of the earliest attempts to impose rational order on alchemical materials and techniques. Al-Biruni preserved biographical details about al-Razi's life and writings that remain essential primary sources.

Al-Razi's legacy in the Hermetic tradition is twofold. As a contemporary of Jabir ibn Hayyan's textual corpus and Ibn Umayl, he belongs to the triad of major Arabic alchemists who transmitted and transformed the Hellenistic alchemical inheritance. His empirical approach to laboratory work helped establish alchemy as a discipline with reproducible procedures, influencing both the later Arabic tradition and the Latin alchemical reception that followed the twelfth-century translation movement.
