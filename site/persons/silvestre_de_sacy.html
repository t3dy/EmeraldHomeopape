Silvestre de Sacy SCHOLAR

late 18th-early 19th c. · 1758–1838 · SCHOLAR

Also known as: Antoine-Isaac Silvestre de Sacy

Antoine-Isaac Silvestre de Sacy (1758–1838) was a French orientalist and one of the founders of modern Arabic and Persian studies in Europe. He held the chair of Arabic at the College de France and trained a generation of scholars who would shape the field of Islamic studies.

Silvestre de Sacy's principal contribution to Hermetic scholarship came in his 1799 study in which he demonstrated the connection between the Arabic Sirr al-Khaliqa (Book of the Secret of Creation) and the legendary figure of Apollonius of Tyana (Balinas in Arabic). His work corrected earlier misidentifications of the text as an Arabic extract of Pliny's Natural History, establishing the correct pseudepigraphic attribution.

This identification was foundational for all subsequent scholarship on the Emerald Tablet and its Arabic context. By correctly linking the Sirr al-Khaliqa to the Apollonius legend, Silvestre de Sacy opened the way for the philological investigations of Julius Ruska, Paul Kraus, and Martin Plessner that would trace the full textual history of the Tablet. His work exemplifies the crucial role of early orientalist scholarship in making the Arabic Hermetic tradition accessible to Western academic study.
