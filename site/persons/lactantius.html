Lactantius AUTHOR

late 3rd-early 4th c. CE · 250–325 · AUTHOR

Also known as: Lucius Caecilius Firmianus Lactantius

Lactantius (c. 250–325 CE) was an early Christian apologist and rhetorician, sometimes called the "Christian Cicero" for his elegant Latin prose. A convert to Christianity who served as tutor to the son of Emperor Constantine, he was one of the first Christian writers to engage extensively with the Hermetic tradition.

In his major work, the Divinae Institutiones (Divine Institutes), Lactantius cited Hermes Trismegistus approvingly as a pagan prophet who had foreseen central Christian truths. He treated the Hermetic writings as evidence that even the wisest pagans had recognized the unity of God, the creation of the world by divine word, and the coming of a final age—doctrines that Lactantius presented as confirmations of Christian teaching.

Lactantius's strategy of appropriating Hermetic authority established a model that would be followed by Christian writers for over a millennium. By treating Hermes as a prophetic witness to Christian truth rather than a rival religious authority, he created a framework that allowed Hermetic texts to circulate within Christian intellectual culture. His approach influenced Augustine's more cautious engagement with the Hermetica and ultimately helped ensure the survival and continued study of Hermetic literature through the medieval period and into the Renaissance.
