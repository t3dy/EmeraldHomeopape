Philip of Tripoli TRANSLATOR

13th c. CE · TRANSLATOR

Also known as: Philippus Tripolitanus

Philip of Tripoli (fl. c. 1220–1243) was a thirteenth-century cleric and translator who produced the full Latin translation of the pseudo-Aristotelian Secretum Secretorum (Secret of Secrets) from the longer Arabic recension. This translation became one of the most widely read and copied texts of the High Middle Ages.

Philip's translation is of particular importance for the history of the Emerald Tablet because the Secretum Secretorum contained a version of the Tablet within its section on alchemy. Because the work was attributed to Aristotle offering counsel to Alexander the Great, it carried enormous intellectual authority in the medieval university system. The Tablet thus entered Latin intellectual culture under the aegis of Aristotelian prestige.

Philip's version complemented and extended the earlier, abridged translation by John of Seville (c. 1120–1140), which had focused on medical and astrological content and did not include the alchemical section. Roger Bacon later composed an extensive commentary on Philip's translation, further enhancing its scholarly prominence. Philip's work thus served as a primary vector for the dissemination of the Emerald Tablet throughout medieval European intellectual culture.
