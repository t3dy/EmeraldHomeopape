Nemesius of Emesa PHILOSOPHER

late 4th c. CE · PHILOSOPHER

Also known as: Nemesius Emesenus

Nemesius of Emesa (fl. late fourth century CE) was a Christian bishop in Syria and the author of De Natura Hominis (On the Nature of Man), a philosophical anthropology composed around 400 CE. His work synthesized Greek philosophical and medical traditions with Christian theology.

Nemesius's central contribution to the intellectual background of the Hermetic tradition lies in his articulation of the human being as a microcosm reflecting the structure of the macrocosm. This concept of macrocosm-microcosm correspondence is widely recognized as the theoretical precursor to the Emerald Tablet's central axiom: that which is above is like that which is below.

Scholars have identified direct textual connections between De Natura Hominis and the Sirr al-Khaliqa (Book of the Secret of Creation) attributed to Pseudo-Apollonius, the Arabic text in which the Emerald Tablet first appears. Paul Kraus demonstrated that the longer version of the Sirr al-Khaliqa includes extracts from Nemesius's work, translated into Arabic as Kitab fi Tabiat al-Insan. This evidence suggests a Syriac-language intermediary stage in the Tablet's transmission and helps establish a terminus post quem for the Sirr al-Khaliqa's composition.
