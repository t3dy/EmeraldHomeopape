Hortulanus COMMENTATOR

early 14th c. CE · COMMENTATOR

Also known as: Ortolanus

Hortulanus (fl. early fourteenth century) is the pseudonymous author of the most widely circulated and influential medieval commentary on the Emerald Tablet, the Liber super textum Hermetis (Commentary on the Text of Hermes). Little is known about his identity beyond the name, which means 'the gardener' in Latin, though he has sometimes been identified with the otherwise obscure alchemist Johannes de Garlandia.

Hortulanus's commentary is remarkable for its systematic interpretation of the Emerald Tablet as a sequential laboratory manual for the creation of the Philosopher's Stone. Working through the Tablet's cryptic aphorisms line by line, Hortulanus decoded each verse as a specific instruction in the alchemical Great Work, translating the Tablet's cosmological and philosophical language into practical procedural terms. This operational reading established the dominant framework through which subsequent alchemists understood the Tablet for centuries.

The legacy of Hortulanus's commentary is defined by its extraordinary longevity and reach. Copied, printed, and translated repeatedly from the fourteenth century onward, it became the standard companion text to the Emerald Tablet itself, ensuring that the Tablet was read primarily through an alchemical-operational lens rather than a purely philosophical or theological one. This interpretive tradition persisted well into the early modern period and shaped the understanding of figures from Roger Bacon's circle through Newton.
