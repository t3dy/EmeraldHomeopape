Cornelius Agrippa AUTHOR

early 16th c. CE · 1486–1535 · AUTHOR

Also known as: Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa von Nettesheim

Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa von Nettesheim (1486-1535) was a German polymath, physician, legal scholar, and occult philosopher who produced the most influential systematic synthesis of Renaissance magical thought. A student of the abbot and cryptographer Johannes Trithemius, Agrippa moved in the intellectual circles of early sixteenth-century humanism while pursuing an ambitious program of integrating Hermetic, Neoplatonic, Kabbalistic, and astrological traditions into a unified magical philosophy.

Agrippa's magnum opus, De occulta philosophia libri tres (Three Books of Occult Philosophy, composed c. 1510, published 1533), organizes the entire domain of magical practice according to a threefold cosmological scheme: natural magic (operating through the elemental world), celestial magic (operating through astral influences), and ceremonial magic (operating through divine and angelic names). The Hermetic doctrine of cosmic sympathy and the macrocosm-microcosm correspondence, drawn from the Corpus Hermeticum and the Asclepius, provides the philosophical foundation for the entire system.

Agrippa's legacy in the Hermetic tradition is immense. The Three Books of Occult Philosophy became the standard reference work for Renaissance and early modern magical practitioners, transmitting Hermetic ideas to audiences far beyond the Latin-reading scholarly elite. His work directly influenced John Dee, Giordano Bruno, and the entire subsequent tradition of Western ceremonial magic, ensuring that Hermetic cosmological principles remained central to esoteric thought well into the modern period.
