Plotinus PHILOSOPHER

3rd c. CE · 204–270 · PHILOSOPHER

Plotinus (c. 204–270 CE) was the Egyptian-born, Rome-based philosopher who founded Neoplatonism, the dominant philosophical school of late antiquity. His Enneads, edited posthumously by his student Porphyry, articulated the emanationist metaphysics that would shape virtually all subsequent Hermetic cosmology.

Plotinus developed a hierarchical model of reality proceeding from the One (the absolute, ineffable source) through Nous (divine intellect) and Psyche (world-soul) down to the material world. This schema provided the philosophical backbone for Hermetic cosmological thinking, paralleling and reinforcing the Hermetic understanding of divine emanation, cosmic sympathy, and the soul's potential ascent back to its divine source.

Although Plotinus himself did not identify as Hermetic and was primarily engaged with interpreting Plato, the conceptual overlap between his system and the Hermetica is substantial. Both traditions emphasize the possibility of mystical union with the divine through an inward ascent of consciousness. Plotinus's student Porphyry and his intellectual successor Iamblichus would develop the implications of his thought in different directions—Porphyry toward rationalist critique of ritual, Iamblichus toward theurgic defense—both of which shaped the reception of Hermeticism in late antiquity and beyond.
