Hermes Trismegistus (Hermes the Thrice-Great) is the syncretic divine-sage figure of Hellenistic Egypt who stands at the center of the entire Hermetic tradition. Arising from the identification of the Greek messenger god Hermes with the Egyptian god of wisdom and writing, Thoth, the figure of Hermes Trismegistus served as the pseudepigraphic author to whom an enormous body of philosophical, alchemical, astrological, and magical texts was attributed from roughly the third century BCE onward.

As the traditional author of the Emerald Tablet, Hermes Trismegistus occupies a uniquely authoritative position in the history of Western esotericism. In the frame narrative of the Kitab Sirr al-Khaliqa, the sage Balinas discovers Hermes's tomb in a subterranean vault, where the body of Hermes sits enthroned, holding an emerald tablet inscribed with the secrets of nature. The texts attributed to Hermes -- including the seventeen treatises of the Corpus Hermeticum, the Latin Asclepius, the Stobaean excerpts, and the Emerald Tablet -- span philosophical theology, cosmology, soteriology, and practical alchemy.

The historical significance of Hermes Trismegistus lies in the extraordinary authority his name conferred. From late antiquity through the Renaissance, Hermes was regarded as a historical contemporary of Moses or even a more ancient sage, a witness to primordial divine revelation (the prisca theologia). This belief, shared by Church Fathers like Lactantius and Augustine and revived by Ficino and Pico, was only dismantled by Isaac Casaubon's philological demonstration in 1614 that the Hermetic texts were post-Christian compositions. Yet even after Casaubon, the figure of Hermes continued to exert profound influence on alchemical, Rosicrucian, and esoteric traditions.
